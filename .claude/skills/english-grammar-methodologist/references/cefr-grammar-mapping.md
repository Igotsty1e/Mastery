# CEFR Grammar Mapping (A1–C2)

This is a working map of which grammatical forms the major Cambridge / Council of Europe references place at which CEFR level. It draws primarily on:

- **English Grammar Profile** (Cambridge English / Cambridge University Press) — the corpus-evidence-based reference for what learners *do* at each level.
- **CEFR Companion Volume with New Descriptors (2020)** — Council of Europe.
- **Cambridge English Qualifications** handbooks (KET/A2 → CPE/C2).
- Cross-checks with **Swan, Practical English Usage (4e)** and **Murphy, English Grammar in Use (5e)**.

Use this map to (a) pick a level-appropriate form when the user only gives a level, (b) flag mismatches when the user requests a form at a level where it doesn't typically appear (productively).

A form's level here = the level at which a learner is *expected to use it productively*. Recognition typically happens one level earlier.

## Quick lookup

### A1 — Breakthrough
- `to be` (am / is / are) — affirmative, negative, questions, short answers.
- Subject pronouns; possessive adjectives (my, your, his/her).
- Articles `a/an` for first mention; basic use of `the`.
- Plural nouns (regular + most common irregulars).
- `this / that / these / those`.
- `there is / there are`.
- Present Simple — affirmative, negative, questions; 3rd person `-s`.
- Present Continuous — for now / current activity.
- `have got` / `have` — possession.
- Modal `can` — ability and basic permission.
- Imperatives.
- Frequency adverbs: always, usually, sometimes, never.
- Adjective + noun order; comparative `-er` with short adjectives (very common ones only).
- Prepositions of place (in, on, at, under, next to) and time (at, on, in for clock/day/month).
- Coordinators `and`, `but`, `or`.
- Wh-questions: what, where, when, who, how (+ how many / much).
- Numbers, dates, times.

### A2 — Waystage
- Past Simple — regular and high-frequency irregulars; affirmative, negative, questions.
- Past Continuous (recognition + simple production for background).
- Present Continuous for future arrangements.
- `going to` for plans and predictions from evidence.
- `will` for spontaneous decisions and simple predictions.
- Comparatives and superlatives (regular + good/bad/far).
- `(not) as … as`.
- Modal verbs: `must` (obligation), `mustn't` (prohibition), `should` (advice), `can/could` (ability past, polite requests), `would like`.
- Quantifiers: some/any, much/many, a lot of, a few/a little.
- Countable vs uncountable basics.
- Possessive `'s` and `of`.
- Adverbs of manner (regular `-ly`).
- Phrasal verbs — most frequent (get up, sit down, turn on/off).
- Prepositions of movement (to, into, out of, through).
- First conditional (real future).
- Connectors: because, so, when, after, before.

### B1 — Threshold
- Present Perfect — `for/since`, `ever/never`, `just/already/yet`; contrast with Past Simple.
- Past Perfect — for sequencing past events.
- Past Continuous vs Past Simple — interrupted action.
- Future forms — full system: `will`, `going to`, present continuous, present simple for timetables.
- Modals — `might / may` (possibility), `have to` vs `must`, `should / shouldn't`, `could` past possibility.
- Second conditional (hypothetical present/future).
- Zero conditional.
- Reported speech — statements and questions with backshift; `say` vs `tell`.
- Passive voice — Present Simple, Past Simple, Future Simple passive.
- Defining relative clauses: `who, which, that, where`.
- `used to` for past habits and states.
- Gerunds and infinitives — common verb patterns (enjoy doing, want to do, like doing/to do).
- Comparatives — `much/a lot/a bit + comparative`; `the + comparative, the + comparative` (recognition).
- Adverbs of degree (quite, fairly, rather — informal).
- Phrasal verbs — wider set, separable vs inseparable.
- Linkers: although, however, while, in order to, so that.

### B2 — Vantage
- Present Perfect Continuous — duration with action focus; vs Present Perfect Simple.
- Past Perfect Continuous.
- Future Continuous, Future Perfect, Future Perfect Continuous.
- Modals of deduction — `must / can't / might / could / may + have + p.p.`
- Third conditional; mixed conditionals (recognition → production).
- Wish / if only — present, past, future.
- Passive — full range incl. modals + passive infinitive (e.g., "is said to be").
- Causative `have / get something done`.
- Reported speech — full backshift, reporting verbs (admit, deny, refuse, suggest, etc. with their patterns).
- Non-defining relative clauses; reduced relative clauses.
- Participle clauses (present and past participles as modifiers).
- Gerunds vs infinitives — verbs that change meaning (remember, stop, try, regret).
- Inversion after negative adverbials (recognition; light production).
- `so / such … that`; `so as to / in order to`.
- Wider linker repertoire: nevertheless, despite, in spite of, whereas.

### C1 — Effective Operational Proficiency
- Inversion — full productive range (Hardly had…, Not only…, Never before…, No sooner…).
- Cleft sentences (it-cleft, what-cleft, all-cleft) — for emphasis and information packaging.
- Fronting and other marked word orders.
- Subjunctive after `suggest, recommend, demand, insist` (especially formal/AmE).
- `be to`-future for formal arrangements / instructions.
- Discourse markers and stance adverbials (arguably, conceivably, supposedly).
- Hedging and approximation language (sort of, kind of, more or less, roughly).
- Ellipsis and substitution (do so, such, the same).
- Complex noun phrases — heavy pre- and post-modification.
- Modality of past speculation and counterfactual reasoning at depth.
- Patterns with `it` as anticipatory subject/object ("I find it strange that…").
- Range of conditional alternatives: `unless, providing/provided that, supposing, on condition that, in case`.
- Nominalisation as a register-shifting device.

### C2 — Mastery
- Full command of the C1 inventory plus:
- Complex clefting and information structuring across paragraphs.
- Stylistic register shifts at will (formal/legal/literary/journalistic/colloquial).
- Idiomatic and proverbial language used naturally and accurately.
- Dialectal and varietal awareness (BrE/AmE/AusE) and code-switching where appropriate.
- Subtle modality and evidentiality.
- Marked syntactic structures used for rhetorical effect.

## Notes on using this map

- **Recognition vs production gap.** A learner at B1 can usually *recognise* B2 forms passively. Don't refuse to put a B2 form in a B1 reading-comprehension prompt — but don't make them produce it.
- **Frequency over inventory.** A1/A2 ≠ "small inventory of forms"; it ≠ small inventory of productive *patterns*. Many forms appear early but only with limited collocations.
- **Lexical level and grammatical level should match.** A B1 grammar exercise written with C1 vocabulary becomes a vocabulary test in disguise. Cross-check vocabulary against English Vocabulary Profile.
- **When in doubt, downgrade by half a level.** A task that's slightly easy reveals control of the form; a task that's slightly hard reveals random guessing.
- **Exam alignment** (rough): A2 ≈ KET, B1 ≈ PET, B2 ≈ FCE, C1 ≈ CAE, C2 ≈ CPE. IELTS bands: 4 ≈ A2/B1, 5–6 ≈ B1/B2, 6.5–7.5 ≈ B2/C1, 8+ ≈ C1/C2.

## When this map is silent

If a form isn't listed, default to checking it in (in this order): English Grammar Profile online (englishprofile.org), Swan's Practical English Usage entry, then Murphy's level for that form. If you still can't pin a level, say so and propose your best guess with reasoning.
