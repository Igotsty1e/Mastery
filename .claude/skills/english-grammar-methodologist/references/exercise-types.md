# Exercise Types — Taxonomy and Construction Recipes

This is the working catalogue of grammar exercise formats this skill produces. Each entry: what the exercise *trains*, when to use it, the construction recipe, common pitfalls, and a minimal example.

The taxonomy is grounded in Penny Ur's *Grammar Practice Activities* and Scott Thornbury's *How to Teach Grammar*, organised here by where the type sits on the **noticing → controlled → meaningful → free** continuum (often abbreviated awareness → accuracy → fluency).

A good lesson typically moves *down* this list. A standalone "drill" sits in the controlled zone. A standalone "task" sits in the meaningful/free zone. When the user asks for a "set" of exercises without specifying types, propose a sequence that crosses zones.

---

## Tier 1 — Awareness / Noticing

Items that make the learner *see* the form before producing it. Often skipped by AI-generated exercises; underrated.

### N1. Identification / underline

**What it trains:** noticing that the form exists in real text. Pre-production.

**When to use:** at the start of a lesson on a new form; for a learner who can produce the form unreliably and needs to consciously re-attend to it.

**Recipe:**
1. Take a short authentic-feeling text (5–8 sentences) where the target form appears 4–6 times.
2. Instruction: "Underline / highlight every example of [form]."
3. Optional follow-up: "What do you notice about when [form] is used?" — invites the learner to articulate the rule.

**Pitfall:** if the form appears too often, the text feels artificial; if too rarely, the noticing fails. 4–6 instances per 80–120 words is the sweet spot.

**Mini-example (Present Perfect, B1):**
> *Read the email. Underline every Present Perfect verb form.*
> "Hi Mark, I've just got home from the airport. I haven't unpacked yet, but I wanted to write before I forgot. The trip has been amazing. We've visited three cities and I've taken about a thousand photos…"

### N2. Sorting / classification

**What it trains:** discriminating the target form from contrasting forms.

**When to use:** to consolidate a contrast (Present Perfect vs Past Simple; *make* vs *do*; *too* vs *enough*).

**Recipe:** give a mixed list of items; ask the learner to sort them into 2 (or 3) buckets.

**Mini-example (B1):** sort 12 sentences into "Present Perfect" or "Past Simple" — and explain *why* in each case.

### N3. Consciousness-raising puzzle (Thornbury)

**What it trains:** rule discovery from data.

**When to use:** with analytic learners; for forms where the rule is more honest stated descriptively than prescriptively (article system; verb + gerund/infinitive patterns).

**Recipe:**
1. Present 6–10 well-chosen examples (some grammatical, some not, marked with ✓/✗).
2. Ask: "What pattern do you see? Try to state the rule."
3. Reveal the rule (or refine the learner's).

## Tier 2 — Controlled practice (accuracy focus)

Items where the answer is largely fixed. Builds form fluency.

### C1. Gap-fill / cloze (open)

**What it trains:** retrieval of the form in a constrained slot.

**When to use:** when the learner knows the form and needs to automate retrieval.

**Recipe:**
1. Write 8–10 sentences with one blank each, in a coherent micro-context (all about the same scenario or character).
2. Provide the bare verb / cue word in brackets after the blank. Example: `She _____ (live) here since 2019.`
3. One unambiguous correct answer per blank. If two are possible, mark which forms are accepted.
4. Avoid blanks where the answer is determinable purely from collocation rather than the target form.

**Pitfall:** blanks where the surrounding clue trivially gives away the answer (e.g., `since` adverb already in the sentence basically forces Present Perfect — fine if that's the point, dangerous if you're trying to assess understanding).

### C2. Multiple-choice (MCQ)

**What it trains:** discrimination between the target form and confusable alternatives.

**When to use:** for diagnosis, exam practice, or when distractors *encode specific misconceptions* you want to surface.

**Recipe:**
1. Stem: a sentence with a blank or a complete sentence with one underlined element.
2. 3–4 options (3 is fine; 4 is exam-standard for FCE/CAE).
3. Distractors should each correspond to a *predictable error* — see `pedagogy-principles.md` § Distractor design.
4. One option must be unambiguously correct.

**Pitfall:** distractors that are obviously wrong (different tense, wrong verb) — they don't discriminate.

**Mini-example (B2 — modals of past deduction):**
> *I tried calling Sarah, but there was no answer. She ____ at work.*
> a) must be — b) must have been ✓ — c) had to be — d) was supposed to be

### C3. Transformation / rewriting (key word transformation)

**What it trains:** form-meaning mapping; structural manipulation. Heavily used in Cambridge exams (especially FCE/CAE Part 4).

**When to use:** when the learner can passively recognise a form but doesn't yet produce it.

**Recipe:**
1. Source sentence in one structure.
2. A "given word" the learner must use, unchanged.
3. The learner rewrites in 2–5 words so meaning is preserved.

**Pitfall:** allowing answers of variable length (give a strict word count window — Cambridge uses 2–5 words).

**Mini-example (B2 — passive):**
> *They are going to build a new bridge here. **GOING***
> A new bridge ___________ here.
> ✓ is going to be built

### C4. Sentence completion

**What it trains:** productive control of the form in a half-fixed context.

**When to use:** as a step up from gap-fill — slightly less constrained.

**Recipe:** provide the start of a sentence; learner finishes it grammatically and meaningfully. Provide a "minimum quality" criterion (e.g., "use at least 5 words" or "use Present Perfect").

**Mini-example (B1 conditional):**
> *If I had more time, ___________.*

### C5. Error correction

**What it trains:** explicit attention to common mistakes; metalinguistic awareness.

**When to use:** review/consolidation; especially powerful for high-frequency persistent errors (e.g., the L1-Russian "He don't like").

**Recipe:**
1. Sentences containing exactly one error each (or "no error" — include 1–2 of these to prevent learners assuming an error always exists).
2. Errors should be *plausible* (the kind a real learner at this level makes), not random typos.
3. Ask the learner to identify and correct.

**Pitfall:** putting in errors no real learner would make ("She *amn't* tired"). See `pedagogy-principles.md` for an L1-aware error catalogue.

### C6. Word order / jumbled sentence

**What it trains:** syntactic structure of the form.

**When to use:** for forms where word order is the main difficulty (questions, indirect questions, negative inversion).

**Recipe:** scramble 6–10 words including the target structure; learner reorders.

**Mini-example (A2 — question word order):** `often / does / what / she / time / get up / ?`

### C7. Matching

**What it trains:** form-meaning or form-form pairing.

**When to use:** for verb + preposition patterns, idioms, collocations, time expressions ↔ tenses.

**Recipe:** two columns; learner draws lines or pairs by index. Make sure each item has exactly one match (or state "some can match more than one").

## Tier 3 — Meaningful practice (form + meaning, semi-controlled)

The learner has a real choice but the choice is constrained by what they want to say.

### M1. Personalisation prompts

**What it trains:** form used to convey real personal meaning.

**When to use:** straight after a controlled drill, to bridge to use.

**Recipe:**
1. Provide 5–8 sentence stems involving the target form.
2. Learner completes them truthfully about themselves.
3. Pair-share or self-write.

**Mini-example (B1 — Present Perfect):**
> *Things I have done this week:*
> *Something I have never tried but want to:*
> *A place I have been to recently:*

### M2. Information-gap

**What it trains:** the form deployed because the learner *needs* to communicate something specific.

**When to use:** for forms tied to specific functions (questions for finding out info; conditionals for negotiating; reported speech for relaying).

**Recipe:** Student A and Student B each have partial info; neither sees the other's; they must use the form to share what they have.

(Often delivered as a pair worksheet; for AI-generated content, render as A/B halves.)

### M3. Picture / visual prompt description

**What it trains:** spontaneous form deployment with a visual scaffold.

**When to use:** with concrete forms (Present Continuous for action; Past Continuous for description; modals of speculation for "what's happening?" pictures).

**Recipe:** describe a picture using at least N target structures.

### M4. Dictogloss

**What it trains:** noticing + reconstruction; integrative form practice.

**When to use:** B1+. Excellent for forms learners use unreliably under pressure.

**Recipe:**
1. Read a short text (4–6 sentences) twice at near-natural speed.
2. Learners take fragmentary notes, then reconstruct in pairs.
3. Compare reconstructions to original; discuss differences.

For AI delivery, present text + comprehension prompts + reconstruction frame.

## Tier 4 — Freer / communicative practice

Form is incidental to a real communicative goal.

### F1. Task-based mini-tasks (TBLT)

**What it trains:** form used in service of a communicative outcome.

**When to use:** as the culmination of a sequence; never as the only practice.

**Recipe:**
1. State the *outcome* (an email, a one-minute talk, a written plan).
2. State the *constraint* that elicits the target form ("you can't be sure, so use modals of speculation"; "this happened in your past, so use Past Simple/Continuous narrative tenses").
3. Provide content support (cue words, picture, scenario).
4. Provide a self-check rubric the learner can use after producing.

### F2. Discussion / opinion prompts

**What it trains:** form deployed in extended discourse.

**When to use:** B2+. Pair with a structural rubric so it stays grammar practice and doesn't drift.

### F3. Roleplay

**What it trains:** form embedded in a functional exchange.

**When to use:** when the form has a clear interactional function (requests, advice, complaints, apologies).

## Choosing a type — quick guide

Asked for a "drill"? → C1 / C3 / C5.
Asked for "exam-style"? → C2 / C3 (Cambridge), or full reading/use of English as appropriate.
Asked for "a worksheet"? → mix: 1 noticing (N1/N3), 2 controlled (C1/C2/C3), 1 meaningful (M1).
Asked for "a lesson on X"? → 1 of each tier, in order.
Asked for "speaking tasks"? → M3 / F1 / F3 with a structural target.
Asked for "homework"? → C1 + C3 + M1 (bite-sized, self-checkable).

## Minimum item counts by type

These are conservative defaults — don't go below unless the user asks:

- Gap-fill: 8 items.
- MCQ: 6–10 items (4 options each).
- Transformation: 6 items.
- Error correction: 6–8 items, with 1–2 "no error" items mixed in.
- Matching: 8–10 pairs.
- Word order: 5–8 items.
- Sentence completion: 5–8 stems.
- Personalisation: 5–8 stems.
