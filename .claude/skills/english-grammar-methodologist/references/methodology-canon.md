# Methodology Canon

This is the shortlist of works that should *back up* anything this skill produces. When generating an exercise, mentally check: "if a Cambridge-trained methodologist read this, would they recognise the rationale in one of these books?" If not, re-do.

The canon is grouped by purpose. For each item: what it is, when to lean on it, the typical thing it tells you that an LLM might otherwise get wrong.

## Reference grammars (use for *correctness* of the form description)

### Michael Swan — *Practical English Usage* (Oxford, 4e)
The desk reference for "is this English actually like this?" Alphabetical entries on individual structures, words, and usage points. Strongly descriptivist, corpus-informed, attentive to register.

- Reach for it when a student question feels like "yes but why this word and not that one" (the entries on `make/do`, `since/for`, articles, modal nuances, reported speech back-shift, etc. are gold).
- Tells you, e.g., that `since` + duration is *not* wrong (`since 2010` and `since two years` differ but the second exists in some varieties), that `should have done` covers both regret and deduction, and that there are far fewer hard rules than schoolbooks claim.

### Raymond Murphy — *English Grammar in Use* / *Essential Grammar in Use* / *Advanced Grammar in Use*
Cambridge's three-tier learner grammar (Essential = elementary/A1–A2, EGiU = intermediate/B1–B2, Advanced = B2–C1+). Each unit = one form, presented as: explanation in plain English, example sentences, then practice exercises.

- Reach for it when you need a *level-appropriate* explanation. Murphy's wording for, say, Present Perfect at B1 is the gold standard: short, concrete, no metalanguage above level.
- Steal his exercise rubrics for inspiration: he is unmatched at writing rubrics learners actually understand on first read.
- Be aware that Murphy is *prescriptive* in the way a good teacher is — simplified for pedagogy. For nuance, cross-check with Swan.

### Martin Hewings — *Advanced Grammar in Use* (companion to Murphy)
For C1+ topics: inversion, cleft, subjunctive, complex passives. Use when generating C1/C2 items.

### Mark Foley & Diane Hall — *MyGrammarLab*
Modern, three-level companion (A1–A2 / B1–B2 / C1–C2). Useful for variety in exercise *types* (more communicative tasks than Murphy).

## Methodology / pedagogy (use for the *shape* of the task)

### Penny Ur — *Grammar Practice Activities* (CUP, 2e)
**The single most useful book this skill leans on.** A typology of grammar exercises with examples, organised by form. Ur is rigorous about what each exercise type *actually trains* and what its limitations are.

- Reach for it when you need an exercise type other than "fill in the blank". Ur is full of variations: ranking, matching, dictogloss, dictation as form practice, completion under time pressure, sentence-building from prompts.
- Pay attention to her distinction between *meaning-focused* and *form-focused* practice — most LLM-produced grammar exercises are over-mechanical because they only do form.

### Penny Ur — *A Course in Language Teaching* (CUP)
The teacher-training reference. Use for: lesson sequencing (PPP and its critiques), error correction philosophy, teaching of specific skills.

### Scott Thornbury — *How to Teach Grammar* (Pearson)
Compact and influential. Sets out three "broad approaches": deductive (rule-then-practice), inductive (examples-then-rule, the noticing approach), and consciousness-raising. Argues that grammar emerges from meaning and that pure deductive teaching produces inert knowledge.

- Reach for it when designing the *first* exercise in a sequence — the one that should make a learner *notice* the form before drilling it.

### Scott Thornbury — *Uncovering Grammar* (Macmillan)
The "grammaring" angle: grammar as a verb, an emergent process, not a set of rules in a box. Especially relevant for: teaching tense/aspect (where rule-based explanations chronically mislead), teaching modality.

### Scott Thornbury — *About Language* (CUP)
Self-study language analysis for teachers. Each chapter dissects one area (tense, aspect, modality, articles, voice). When you're about to write items on, say, the article system at C1 — read this first if you can.

### Diane Larsen-Freeman — *The Grammar Book* (with Marianne Celce-Murcia, Heinle)
Comprehensive descriptive grammar with pedagogical commentary. Stronger than Swan on AmE; valuable when working with US-aligned curricula.

### Diane Larsen-Freeman — *Teaching Language: From Grammar to Grammaring* (Heinle)
The theoretical case for treating grammar as a fifth skill (alongside reading/writing/listening/speaking). Useful framing when designing *productive*, freer-practice items.

### Jim Scrivener — *Teaching English Grammar* (Macmillan)
Practical "what works in the classroom" reference. Lots of activity ideas with the "why" attached.

### Jim Scrivener — *Learning Teaching* (Macmillan, 3e)
The all-purpose teacher-training reference. Reach for it when designing the broader lesson architecture, not just the grammar slot.

### Jeremy Harmer — *The Practice of English Language Teaching* (Pearson, 5e)
The other all-purpose teacher-training reference. Particularly good on the ESA model (Engage, Study, Activate) and on managing controlled-to-free practice transitions.

### Jack C. Richards & Theodore S. Rodgers — *Approaches and Methods in Language Teaching* (CUP)
The historical map of methods (Grammar Translation → Audio-Lingual → CLT → TBLT → Lexical Approach → Content-Based → Dogme). Use when a project asks you to align with a specific methodology.

## Corpora and frequency references (use to ground items in real English)

### English Grammar Profile (Cambridge)
The corpus-evidence-based reference for what learners *do* at each CEFR level. Online: englishprofile.org. The single most authoritative source for "is this form A2 or B1?"

### English Vocabulary Profile (Cambridge)
The lexical companion. Cross-check the *vocabulary* in your items against the level — easy to slip C1 vocabulary into a B1 grammar exercise.

### CEFR Companion Volume with New Descriptors (2020)
Official Council of Europe document. The level descriptors for each skill at each level. Use when justifying level choice in design notes.

### COCA (Corpus of Contemporary American English) / BNC (British National Corpus)
For checking whether a phrasing is naturally attested. When in doubt about whether real speakers say something, search before you write.

## When to lean on whom — quick decision rule

| Question | Default authority |
|---|---|
| Is this English actually correct/natural? | Swan, then COCA/BNC |
| How do I explain this form at level X? | Murphy (or Hewings for C1+) |
| What CEFR level does this form belong to? | English Grammar Profile, then EVP for vocab |
| What kind of *exercise* should I write? | Ur (Grammar Practice Activities) |
| Should the first task be deductive or inductive? | Thornbury (How to Teach Grammar) |
| How do I sequence this within a lesson? | Scrivener / Harmer |
| Am I doing the right thing methodologically? | Larsen-Freeman / Richards & Rodgers |

## What to do if these references disagree

This happens, especially between Swan (descriptive) and Murphy (pedagogically simplified). Default rule: **for the items themselves, follow Swan**; **for the explanations and rubrics shown to learners, follow Murphy's register**. This combination — descriptive accuracy + pedagogical clarity — is what a well-trained methodologist does.
